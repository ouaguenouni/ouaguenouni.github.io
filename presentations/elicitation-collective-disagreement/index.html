Plurality matrix & level

Plurality matrix. \(p_S^{\pi}(a) := \Pr_\pi(a \text{ ranked first within } S)\). Rows grouped by \(|S|\) form the degree (degree 2 = pairwise).
Level. A measure has level \(k\) if it can be expressed from degree \(\leq k\) data, but not from degree \(\leq k-1\).

Proposition. The rank variance is a measure of level 3: \[ \mathrm{Var}(a) \;=\; \sum_{b\neq a} p_{ab}\,(1-p_{ab}) \;+\!\! \sum_{b\neq c} \big(p_{abc}(a) - p_{ab}\,p_{ac}\big). \]

Proof. Let \(X_b := \mathbb{1}[a\succ b]\), so \(r_a = m - \sum_{b\neq a} X_b\). Then

\[ \mathrm{Var}(r_a) = \mathbb{E}\big[(\textstyle\sum_b X_b)^2\big] - \big(\mathbb{E}[\textstyle\sum_b X_b]\big)^2, \] \[ \mathbb{E}\big[(\textstyle\sum_b X_b)^2\big] = \sum_b \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[X_b^2]}_{p_{ab}} + \sum_{b\neq c} \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[X_b X_c]}_{p_{abc}(a)}, \qquad \big(\mathbb{E}[\textstyle\sum_b X_b]\big)^2 = \sum_{b,c} p_{ab}\,p_{ac}. \]

Collecting terms gives the formula above — degree 2 plus degree 3 entries.\(\quad\square\)

Proof of the rank-variance proposition; the closed form mixes degree-2 and degree-3 plurality entries, so the measure is level 3. Skippable (press right to leave, down for the divisiveness proof).

Proposition. The divisiveness is a measure of level 3: \[ \mathrm{Div}(a) = \frac{1}{m-1}\sum_{b\neq a} \Big| 1 + \!\!\sum_{c\neq a,b}\!\! \Big[ p_{abc}(a)\Big(\tfrac{1}{1-p_{ab}} + \tfrac{1}{p_{ab}}\Big) - \tfrac{p_{ac}}{1-p_{ab}} \Big] \Big|. \]

Proof. Splitting voters by their verdict on \(\{a,b\}\), divisiveness is the gap in \(a\)'s mean rank between the two camps: \[ \mathrm{Div}(a) = \frac{1}{m-1}\sum_{b\neq a} \big| \mathbb{E}[r_a \mid b\succ a] - \mathbb{E}[r_a \mid a\succ b] \big|. \]

\[ \mathbb{E}[r_a \mid b\succ a] = m + \frac{1}{1-p_{ab}}\!\!\sum_{c\neq a,b}\!\!\big(p_{abc}(a) - p_{ac}\big), \qquad \mathbb{E}[r_a \mid a\succ b] = m - 1 - \frac{1}{p_{ab}}\!\!\sum_{c\neq a,b}\!\! p_{abc}(a). \]

Substituting both back gives the formula above — degree 2 and degree 3 entries.\(\quad\square\)

Proof of the divisiveness proposition: each conditional Borda score equals m minus a conditional mean rank; inclusion-exclusion gives the two conditional expectations, whose substitution yields a degree-2/degree-3 closed form, so level 3. Skippable.

Moments of the rank distribution

Central moment. For an alternative \(a\) with mean rank \(\mu_a = \mathbb{E}_\pi[r_a]\), the \(k\)-th central moment is \(\; M_k(a) := \mathbb{E}_\pi\big[(r_a - \mu_a)^k\big].\)
First moment. \(\;\mu_a = 1 + \sum_{b\neq a}(1 - p_{ab}) = m - \sum_{b\neq a} p_{ab} = m - \mathrm{Bor}(a)\)⇒proportional to the Borda score.
Second central moment. \(\; M_2(a) = \mathrm{Var}(a)\)⇒the rank variance (level 3).
Proposition. The \(k\)-th central moment is a measure of level \(k+1\): \[ M_k(a) = (-1)^k \sum_{s=0}^{k} c_s(a) \!\!\sum_{\substack{S\subseteq\mathcal{A}\setminus\{a\}\\ |S|=s}}\!\! p_{S\cup\{a\}}(a), \]

where \(\;c_s(a) = \sum_{j=0}^{s} (-1)^{s-j}\binom{s}{j}\big(j - \mathrm{Bor}(a)\big)^{k}.\)

⇒skewness is level 4,   excess kurtosis is level 5.
Define the central moments of the rank distribution. The first moment is the mean rank m minus the Borda score (degree-2, level 2). The second central moment is the rank variance (level 3). The general proposition: the k-th central moment has a closed form in plurality entries of degree up to k+1, and its level is exactly k+1 — so the hierarchy never stops; skewness is level 4 and excess kurtosis is level 5.

Eliciting the plurality matrix

In practice we estimate the matrix by querying sampled voters. In a digital-democracy setting, the protocol must respect two principles.

Anonymous sampling. every query draws a voter — a single preference order — from the population according to the profile \(\pi\); the voter is their preferences, never identified.

We then judge a protocol by two costs: the observed population \(N\) — how many voters we may query — and the cognitive load we place on each.

It is easier to find 10 000 people who each answer 3 questions than 100 who answer 20.

Maximum cognitive load. the heaviest single query: \(\;\mathrm{MCL} := \max_{j} c_j\), where \(c_j\) is the number of pairwise comparisons asked of voter \(j\).

Budget. the total work over the \(N\) queried voters: \(\;B := \sum_{j} c_j\).

Tied by the population — at equal load per voter, \(\;B = N\cdot\mathrm{MCL}\). For a fixed budget, fewer questions per voter \(\iff\) more voters.

Frame elicitation: we estimate the plurality matrix by querying sampled voters. Two principles for a digital democracy. (1) Anonymous sampling — a voter is a preference order drawn from the population per the profile π; we don't track who. (2) Two costs: the observed population N and the cognitive load per voter — which we want low. Motivation: 10 000 people answering 3 questions each is more realistic than 100 answering 20. Definitions: maximum cognitive load MCL = the heaviest single query (max comparisons asked of any voter); budget B = total comparisons; with equal per-voter load they tie via B = N·MCL, so at a fixed budget reducing the load per voter buys more voters.

Two elicitation primitives

\(k\)-Chain

\(k-1\) sequential pairwise comparisons: compare \(a_1, a_2\), then the running winner against \(a_3,\dots,a_k\).

Cognitive load · one voter \(k-1\) comparisons

Independent samples at degree \(\ell\) \(1\) — the winner of the size-\(\ell\) prefix

\(k\)-Ranking

a full ranking of the \(k\) alternatives, elicited by pairwise comparisons.

Cognitive load · one voter \(\lceil\log_2 k!\rceil \approx k\log k\) comparisons

Independent samples at degree \(\ell\) \(\binom{k}{\ell}\) — every \(\ell\)-subset's winner, by transitivity

A clear trade-off: the chain is light per voter but yields a single sample; the ranking yields \(\binom{k}{\ell}\) samples — at a higher cognitive cost.

Two primitives, side by side. A k-chain: k−1 sequential comparisons (winner stays), costing k−1 per voter, giving 1 sample at each degree (the nested prefixes). A k-ranking: a full ranking of the k items, costing ~k log k comparisons, but by transitivity it gives C(k,ℓ) independent samples at degree ℓ (the winner of every ℓ-subset). So: chain = cheap but few samples; ranking = many samples but heavy — a trade-off.

Population vs cognitive load

For a fixed degree, the population and the per-voter load are tied by a precise law.

Theorem. Fix a degree \(k\). To estimate every degree-\(k\) entry of the plurality matrix to accuracy \(\varepsilon\) with probability \(1-\delta\):

  • a \(k\)-chain needs \(N = \binom{m}{k}\,T_k\) voters, at \(\mathrm{MCL} = k-1\);
  • a \(k\)-ranking needs \(N = \dbinom{m}{k}\,T_k \big/ (k\log k)\) voters, at \(\mathrm{MCL} = k\log k\).

where \(T_k = \ln(2Q_k/\delta)/(2\varepsilon^2)\) and \(Q_k = k\binom{m}{k}\) is the number of degree-\(k\) entries.

The ranking cuts the population by a factor \(k\log k\) — at a higher load per voter. More cognitive load per voter buys fewer voters.

The formal trade-off. For a fixed degree k, estimating all degree-k plurality entries to ε with confidence 1−δ: a k-chain needs C(m,k)·T_k voters at MCL k−1; a k-ranking needs the same divided by k log k voters, at MCL k log k. T_k is the per-entry sample count from Hoeffding + union bound. So rankings cut the population by k log k at the cost of a heavier query — population and cognitive load trade off, which the next (interactive) figure lets you explore.
