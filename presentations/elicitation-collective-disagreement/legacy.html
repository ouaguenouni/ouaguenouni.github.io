Motivation

Most of social choice asks one question: given the votes, which alternative wins?

Aggregation rules select the most preferred alternatives from declared preferences.

Motivation

Agreement is half the story. A population also has to face its disagreements.

On Pol.is, the proposals with the narrowest margins are surfaced next to the consensual ones.

Waldron: a society confronts and resolves disagreements before reaching agreement. Pol.is highlights divisive proposals alongside consensual ones.

Motivation

Some disagreement is structural: the population splits into camps.

Identifying that structure is the goal.

An alternative is divisive when one group favours it while another opposes it.

The tension

  • Full rankings reveal everything but are costly to collect.
  • Pairwise comparisons are cheap and intuitive.
  • Pairwise comparisons miss the structure of disagreement.
Surveys ask pairwise comparisons (simple) or full rankings (complete but costly); pairwise cannot separate structural disagreement from noise.

This talk

Two questions, treated together:

  • What aggregate data is needed to measure disagreement?
  • How do we elicit it under a query budget?
We find the minimal aggregated preference information for several disagreement measures, then design protocols to elicit it.

Concepts and notation

Alternatives. A finite set \(\mathcal{A}=\{a_1,\dots,a_m\}\), with \(m \geq 3\).

The options voters rank: candidates, proposals, items.

Concepts and notation

Voter. A strict ranking \(\succ\) of \(\mathcal{A}\).

For example \(a \succ b \succ c\): \(a\) first, \(c\) last.

Each voter holds a strict linear order over the alternatives.

Concepts and notation

Profile. A distribution \(\pi\) over the \(m!\) rankings.

Example over \(\{a,b,c\}\): \(\quad \pi = \tfrac12\,(a \succ b \succ c) + \tfrac12\,(c \succ b \succ a)\).

A profile is a probability distribution over rankings; a voter is a ranking drawn from it.

Concepts and notation

Definition (rank).

\[ r_a \;:=\; 1 + |\{\, b \neq a : b \succ a \,\}| \]

The position of \(a\) in a voter's ranking; \(r_a = 1\) means first.

The rank of a counts how many alternatives beat it, plus one.

Key shift

An alternative does not have one rank.

Across voters, \(a\) holds a distribution of ranks, \(\Pr[r_a = i]\).

Disagreement about a is a property of this rank distribution.

Pairwise data

For two alternatives, \(\;p_{xy} := \Pr[x \succ y]\).

The aggregate of all pairwise comparisons. The standard, low-cost query.

Pairwise proportions are degree-2 data and underlie many voting rules.

All pairwise proportions coincide: \(\;\; p_{ab}=p_{ba}=p_{ac}=p_{ca}=p_{bc}=p_{cb}=\tfrac12\).

Same pairwise data, opposite rank structure.

The antagonism profile shares every pairwise proportion with impartial culture, yet splits the rank of a between first and last.

To see the difference, we need data that lives above the pairwise level.

The two profiles differ only in higher-order statistics; this motivates the plurality matrix.

The plurality matrix

For a subset \(S\) and \(a \in S\):

\[ p_S^{\pi}(a) \;:=\; \Pr_\pi(a \text{ is ranked first within } S) \]

The matrix entry records, for each subset S, the probability that a tops S.

Degree

Group the rows by subset size \(|S|\).

  • Degree 2: pairwise comparisons.
  • Degree \(m\): plurality scores.

No degree can be recovered from another.

Degree 2 is pairwise; degree m is the proportion ranking each alternative first.

Level of a measure

A statistic has level \(k\) when degree \(\leq k\) data expresses it, and degree \(\leq k-1\) does not.

The smallest subset size needed to compute it.

Level is the minimal degree required to express a measure.

Three measures

Agreement index \(A(\pi)\): how far each pair is from a tie, averaged.

\[ A(\pi) = \tbinom{m}{2}^{-1}\!\!\sum_{\{x,y\}} \big|\,2\,p_{xy}-1\,\big| \]

Level 2.

The agreement index ranges from 0 (all pairs split) to 1 (unanimity), and uses only pairwise data.

Three measures

Rank variance of \(a\): how much its rank moves across voters.

\[ \mathrm{Var}(a) = \mathbb{E}\big[(r_a - \mathbb{E}[r_a])^2\big] \]

Level 3.

Rank variance is a level-3 measure; the closed form uses degree-2 and degree-3 entries.

Three measures

Divisiveness of \(a\): split the voters on each pair \((a,b)\), then compare how high \(a\) ranks on average (its Borda score) in the two camps.

Level 3.

Divisiveness compares the Borda score of a among voters preferring a to b versus the reverse, averaged over b; it is level 3.

Rank variance and divisiveness are level 3.

Pairwise comparisons alone cannot recover them.

Impartial culture and antagonism share all degree-2 data yet differ in variance and divisiveness.

The level hierarchy

Level \(\ell\) = what \(\ell\)-wise data tells you about the rank distribution.

Each degree of the plurality matrix reads a different part of the rank distribution.

Hierarchy

Pairwise data fixes the mean rank:

\[ \mathbb{E}[r_a] = m - \mathrm{Bor}(a) \]

The Borda score is a level-2 measure.

The expected rank equals m minus the Borda score, which is degree-2 data.

Hierarchy

Pairwise data fixes the mean, and nothing above it.

The variance already needs degree 3.

The mean is pinned at degree 2; the variance is the first quantity that requires degree 3.

Central moments

\[ M_k(a) = \mathbb{E}\big[(r_a - \mathbb{E}[r_a])^k\big] \]

\(M_k\) is a measure of level \(k+1\).

The k-th central moment of the rank has level exactly k+1.

Reading the moments

Skewness \(\gamma_1\): asymmetry of the rank distribution.

Level 4.

Skewness measures the asymmetry of the distribution of the rank of a.

Reading the moments

Excess kurtosis \(\gamma_2\): tail extremity, the tendency to produce outliers.

Level 5.

Excess kurtosis relates to tail extremity.

\((\gamma_1, \gamma_2)\) is a two-dimensional summary of the rank distribution.

No level-2 or level-3 measure can capture it.

Skewness and excess kurtosis together give a 2D summary invisible to lower levels.

The hierarchy is strict

For every \(k \geq 2\), there are two profiles \(\pi, \pi'\) with

\( p_S^{\pi}(a) = p_S^{\pi'}(a)\) for all \(|S| \leq k\),   yet they differ at some \(|T| = k+1\).

No measure of level \(k\) tells them apart.

Backup · proof sketch

Set \(m = d+2\) (the separating degree \(d\) plays the role of \(k\)). Profile \(\pi_w\): draw \(r_a\) from a distribution \(w\) on positions, place the rest uniformly.

\[ p_S^{\pi_w}(a) = \sum_{j=1}^{m} w_j\,\frac{\binom{m-j}{\,s-1}}{\binom{m-1}{\,s-1}} \]

Linear in \(w\), depending on \(S\) only through \(s=|S|\). Matching up to degree \(d\) is \(d\) equations of distinct polynomial degree (rank \(d\)); the degree-\((d{+}1)\) separation is a degree-\(d\) polynomial, not implied.

Witness \(d=3,\,m=5\): \(w\) uniform, \(w'=\tfrac{1}{20}(3,7,1,5,4)\). Then \(p_S(a)=1/|S|\) for \(|S|\in\{2,3\}\); at \(|S|=4\), \(1/4\) versus \(19/80\).

Every level is occupied.

The \(k\)-central moment is a measure of level exactly \(k+1\).

Moments give a meaningful measure at every level, so the hierarchy does not collapse on its own.

When structure helps

Under Plackett-Luce (latent strengths) or single-peaked (options on a line) preferences, every degree is a function of degree 2.

The hierarchy collapses to level 2.

Single-peaked needs the axis known in advance; Plackett-Luce needs only pairwise comparisons.

Plackett-Luce needs no access to the latent strengths; single-peaked needs the axis known in advance.

Eliciting the matrix

Estimate every entry to accuracy \(\varepsilon\) with probability \(1-\delta\).

Degree 2 is just pairwise comparisons. Degree \(\geq 3\) needs a richer query.

We take a PAC view: epsilon-approximate each entry with probability one minus delta.

Primitive

\(k\)-chain. Compare \(a_1, a_2\), then the running winner against each next alternative.

\(\;|S|-1\) pairwise comparisons. Lowest cognitive load.

The chain reports nested-prefix winners, one sample per degree, with k minus 1 comparisons.

Primitive

\(k\)-ranking. Rank the whole subset \(S\).

\(\;\Theta(k \log k)\) comparisons. By transitivity, a ranking of \(S\) fixes the winner of every subset: \(\binom{|S|}{k}\) samples at each degree \(k\).

A full ranking of S determines the top of every subset of S, giving many samples at higher cognitive load.

Two cost axes

For a protocol of length \(N\) with \(c_j\) comparisons at step \(j\):

\( B = \sum_{j} c_j \qquad \mathrm{MCL} = \max_j c_j \)

  • Budget \(B\): total comparisons.
  • Max cognitive load \(\mathrm{MCL}\): the heaviest single query.

Down for the budget derivation.

Budget is the total cost; MCL is the cost of the heaviest query, from one comparison to a full ranking.

Backup · budget

Degree-\(k\) entries: \(Q_k = k\binom{m}{k}\). Per-entry samples (Hoeffding + union bound): \(T_k = \dfrac{\ln(2Q_k/\delta)}{2\varepsilon^2}\).

  • \(k\)-chain: \(N_{\mathrm{chain}} = \binom{m}{k} T_k\) voters, \(\mathrm{MCL}=k-1\).
  • \(k\)-ranking: \(N_{\mathrm{rank}} = \dfrac{\binom{m}{k} T_k}{k\log k}\) voters, \(\mathrm{MCL}=k\log k\).

Backup · sampling without replacement

Each voter answers once, so samples are drawn without replacement. Serfling sharpens Hoeffding:

\( T_\ell' = T_\ell\,\dfrac{n+1}{n+T_\ell} \).

When \(n \geq N_{\mathrm{chain}}\) the reduction stays below \(2\%\) once \(\binom{m}{\ell}\geq 50\), so the paper keeps the Hoeffding bound.

At a fixed budget, comparisons per voter trade off against the number of voters.

Chains and rankings sit at the two ends.

At a fixed budget, cognitive load per voter trades against the number of voters.

A limit

Any protocol that reaches degree \(k\) needs \(\mathrm{MCL} \geq k-1\).

Chains achieve it. So a level-3 measure forces at least one voter to compare three alternatives jointly.

Identifying the top of a size-k subset needs at least k minus 1 comparisons; chains are optimal in MCL.

Budget against cognitive load

Chains are optimal at low MCL or large population; rankings become optimal otherwise.

Two regimes on one frontier.

  • Digital democracy: low load, many participants. Use chains.
  • Expert panel: high load, few participants. Use rankings.
Low-MCL chains suit consultations where attention is scarce; high-MCL rankings suit expert settings.

Limitations

  • The matrix records only "tops a subset" events; some measures fall outside it.
  • Voters are i.i.d., truthful, noiseless; load is counted in pairwise comparisons.
  • Experiments use voters who gave complete rankings.
The k-Kemeny family lies outside the framework; the assumptions are standard but restrictive.
